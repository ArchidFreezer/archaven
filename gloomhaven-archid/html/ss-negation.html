Soothsinger Negation

Overview

The Soothsinger is, in my opinion, the most versatile class in the game. No matter your party composition, no matter the scenario or situation, no matter the stage of character development or difficulty of the game/campaign, she is always going to have many options available to her to make a big impact. This makes her a blast to play. Playing her, you'll never feel like you're weak, or not useful in a specific party or scenario. Also, all your party members will love having you around because of what you bring to the party.

Before I start, let me set the stage:

  1. This guide is for playing Soothsinger as what is broadly called a 'support' character. I'm going to assume you have at least one other primary damage dealer in you party and that you aren't expected to contribute directly to attacking. That's the only thing you'll need -- a tank is neither needed nor assumed.
  2. I play the Soothsinger in 3-4 character parties. In a 2 character party, you may need to have slightly more offensive focus if your partner is a medium damage dealer only.
  3. This guide is written with the 'late-game' in mind (high character level, high scenario difficulty level). The Soothsinger is a very powerful and versatile classes at all stages of the game, and you can get away with doing almost anything you want. As a result, it is only as the game gets much harder that you need a more focussed strategy, and this is what this guide is for.

When I refer to 'late-game' in this guide, this refers loosely to situations where monsters begin to become very strong, and start to make modest numbers like Attack 3 or Shield 1-2 look relatively insignificant. This generally comes as you approach character level 9, but can come earlier if you start to bump the scenario difficulty higher than the recommended level.

I've played a lot of the Soothsinger to great effect at scenario difficulty 6-7, so those are my credentials.

An important tip top note with Songs: always pay attention when you rest to whether you should bring the Song back or not. If bringing the Song back will result in an even hand size, you'll be netting a turn of longevity, which is very welcome on this low-longevity class. Additionally, as you lack top actions, many times you don't even mind spending your top action for the turn (re)playing a Song, doubly-so if you have your solo item. Obviously if you're in the middle of some really intense combat, you're usually better off not bringing the Song back so that you can immediately play a top CC action, so you do need to read the situation.

Main Elements of Negation Gameplay

Supporting and Negating

From here on out, I'm going to split up the broad idea of 'support' into two. The Soothsinger excels at two things:
  1. 'Support' abilities: supporting allies directly in terms of defence, offence and utility, usually by providing passive song bonuses or active bonuses.
  2. 'Negation' abilities: negating monster effectiveness, mainly through: (i) crowd control abilities (stun/disarm); and (ii) curses.

Both supporting and negating are useful, and what's great is that the Soothsinger has so many types of each in her arsenal. For the most part, you can focus on whichever you find more interesting and you'll get by.

Changing with Monster Levels

As you start to approach late-game, the equation starts to change. Almost all of the Soothsinger's supporting abilities are numerically based, meaning they have set numerical values.

Examples:

  • In defence: her shield song (Defensive Ditty [1]) gives Shield 1 to all allies, and this isn't going ever to improve.
  • In offence: she can make an ally perform an Attack 4 (Call to Action [1]), and this particular card isn't getting any stronger. Or her +1 attack song (Power Ballad [1]) will give, of course, +1 attack, and no more.

This makes her supporting abilities weaker as enemies get stronger. That Shield 1 is great when enemies are hitting you with Attack 2 or 3, by mitigating 33-50% of damage on average. When the enemies are hitting you with Attack 5 or 6, that Shield 1 is starting to look less useful, mitigating only 16.6-20% of damage on average. It's still better than nothing, of course, but there's the opportunity cost of not playing a better card instead

In contrast, negating abilities scale completely with the strength of monsters. A stun or disarm, stops a monster from attacking at all. It mitigates 100% of any given attack, no matter how big. The same is true for curses. As a result, the Soothsinger's negating abilities are what makes her really shine in the late-game. At the same time, it is just as effective at early to mid-game, so you lose nothing by investing in the late-game and focusing on negation.

Build Philosophy

  1. As explained above, we prioritise negating ability cards over supporting ability cards.
  2. Pick one or two songs which suit your party/scenario, and stick to them. In 90% of situations, I use the same song for a whole scenario (picking it up when resting if needed to get an even-numbered card hand, in order to maximise the number of turns, of course). There is no need to bring extra song cards, 'just in case' the song is useful. As a result, you're able to bring some cards with powerful active (non-song) top-side actions. This means that switching songs often is a big opportunity cost, since you give up a powerful active top-side action, so this provides more reason to stick with one song. Think of the song as a nice constant passive bonus, but no more -- the active negating abilities are your most important actions.
  3. Many of the Soothsinger's most powerful active actions are bottom-side actions. This means you don't get to move often. But when you do move, make it count, so that you're in the best position to do a powerful bottom-side action the next turn or two. This means we need to have a couple big move cards in our deck. I will refer to these two cards as your two 'primary' move cards. This may be supplemented by slightly smaller 'secondary' move cards.
  4. The Soothsinger has a small hand size of 9, and has so many immensely powerful non-loss cards (some are more powerful than the loss actions of many other classes). As a result, we want to maximise the longevity of her ability to play these cards repeatedly. For the Soothsinger, no action is worth losing a card over, unless it's for a critical moment in very situation-specific situations, so we aren't going to pick a card just for its loss action.

In summary, we are going to pick our cards when we level up with the above things in mind. This means that: (i) we will choose negating cards over non-scaling supporting cards; (ii) skip songs which will get replaced later on, or which are outclassed by a non-song card that level, and prefer songs that aren't very situationally dependent; (iii) give high priority to big move cards; (iv) generally ignore the lose actions on cards.

Unending Chant is our first legitimately underwhelming card. A top loss on a class with 9 cards that also sometimes leaves persistent cards up and loses longevity accordingly would need to be really powerful.

The biggest problem, however, is just one of initiative. Shield is only good if you go before enemies and this card, inexplicably, has horrible initiative. On top of that, you simply don't have non-Song top actions with good initiative; you won't play Songs many times in a scenario so you can't count on them for initiative and your bottom actions with good initiative aren't going to help you play this card any more effectively. The best use would be the first turn of a scenario, combined with Defensive Ditty for initiative, but that's still pretty narrow for this card unless you can guarantee a long rest afterwards and on top of that, first rooms will typically put you in a spot where you don't need to move, in which case you have a ton of better bottom actions.

Having to get into melee in order to do an Immobilize Attack 3 as a squishy ranged character is just not worth it in terms of the risk/reward. Even with our great modifier deck, this just isn't the action you're looking for (outside of 2p, where it can be better, but again this guide is not for 2p) and you'll have much better actions by the time you get a good enough modifier deck.

Meanwhile, the bottom has pretty much all of the same problems as Unending Chant only magnified because it's a loss on a class that really wants to avoid losses because of hand size.

Warding Dagger continues the trend of underwhelming cards.

And the bottom is kind of funny, making a typical bottom a bit better by adding an Attack. You'll never be happy to bring this bottom outside of scenarios where you're required to bring a Loot action, but on those scenarios you'll definitely be happy to have the added attack (especially as your modifier deck improves).

Wistful Wounding is our worst Song yet although the bar was quite high with two that were very good generically and one that was excellent situationally.

The idea is just to switch this on for 1-2 turns and attack a bunch of different targets but first of all, that's highly situational for a card that also has a pretty situational bottom and that actively encourages bad targeting (it's typically much better to focus-fire enemies to eliminate incoming damage as quickly as possible). The biggest advantage this has over Power Ballad as an offensive song is that it does work with AOE attacks so it's possible this might work in some parties that are something like Soothsinger + rarely-attacking tank + low-damage, multiple non-loss large AOE attacks ranged class such as the Quartermaster with their level 6 card.

The real meat of Melody and Harmony is the top action, which is just excellent for us. We don't get a lot of opportunities to attack safely from range with the Soothsinger but our modifier deck is so excellent that we need to take any we can. An Attack 3 for us becomes much more akin to an Attack 5 + bonus, and getting that while still moving is awesome. Still, it can be an excellent sideboard card, even for the Comfy Shoes build, to tackle certain scenarios. We won't take it immediately, up against Melody, but we will consider it down the road.

Meanwhile, the bottom is an okay loss with a lot of summons but still has the same issues other losses had for us and it's not very often that the lost longevity and thus lost turns of playing Provoke Terror and Dirge will be made up for by the effect, outside of boss scenarios where it can be quite decent.

On its own, I've found Proc to be relatively unimpressive after a reasonable amount of testing. But if you have party members who can buff all attacks for the round (for example, the Tinkerer with Enhancement Field), this action can quickly get out of control.

Allows us to spam curses at a rate of three a round at the start of a scenario to get a head start before switching to Dirge.

The bottom of Nightmare Serenade is also great. Consistently adding two Curses while also letting us deal a very respectable amount of damage by flipping two modifiers, this is an appealing bottom Attack action, even when we have others like Dirge and Aria.

Nightmare Serenade is a Song we're only going to be using at the start of a scenario to top up the curse levels.

With our boots, it can otherwise be a reasonable-initiative Move 3 for when we don't have good targeting with the attack.

New Cards

The top does give us another top action which can be reasonably-powerful in the right party.

Commanding Presence has a laughably-bad bottom loss for a level 8 card on this class.

If you have an ally who will typically move into the middle of a large group of enemies safely then this will give you another top action that will do a fair amount of work. Even with that sort of ally though, you shouldn't bring this on scenarios with mostly beefy enemies.

The Song on Trill, however, can be very useful for healing when we're out of combat (during combat, Dirge or Ditty will typically be more effective).

Tranquil Trill's bottom is exceptionally difficult for us to use effectively, even with our solo scenario item, and thus isn't very appealing.

Thus, if our party lacks healing, Trill is a very reasonable choice.

New Cards

First of all, the top Song is very good, a significant upgrade to Power Ballad, although not all parties will notice the difference. If your party has a lot of AOE attacks, however, you'll find yourself wanting to activate this often enough.

The biggest dissuasion from using the top comes in the form of the bottom action (although you still can and should use the top sometimes with certain allies). This bottom action is just insane - easily Stunning 2-3 targets each time with a non-loss action.

Captivating Performance doesn't make our life any easier, but the sheer power level of this card at least makes the choice easy.

Combining this bottom and Provoke Terror will all you to Stun the vast majority of most rooms in most scenarios, which is just absurd for one character to be able to do without spending any losses.

Having such a large top move works well to allow us to position ourselves for our non-move bottom actions. And having a nice added bonus of being able to make some number of enemies attack other enemies is really sweet.

The bottom is pretty powerful but is by far less compelling than the top, even though it's a ton of bottom damage, just because it has some very strict constraints in order to actually yield worthwhile damage, and it competes with our better half of actions, which the top complements instead.

Shadow Puppets is just a nice card to have. With our limited bottom movement, a large top move is pretty great.

Finally, the worst part of this card is the initiative, which is quite bad. That being said, we're pretty fine on initiative, so it's not the end of the world.
