Brute Striker

This build is a basic striker build where the focus is on moving and attacking, there are some combos that may be performed at higher prosperity, but the basics remain the same.

There is some built in tankiness to the brute but we will not be leaning into that in this build.

So why would you play a Striker Build?

  • Solid damage dealer who can act as on off-tank.
  • Has some minor CC abilities, but very limited in number.

The Striker build loves the following:

  • Party members who can do some ranged damage.
  • Support party members and those with some hard CC.

Build Deck

New Cards

Balanced Measure represents an Attack 4 or 6 non-loss at level 1 for this class, which is certainly one of the best things we can do, by far.

The bottom is also part of higher level combos that are good on boss fights using a level 6 card which we'll talk about at level 6.

Ah, Balanced Measure, THE Brute card. Ignore the fact that this card is supposed to be a 'level X', card rather than 'level 1', it's as core of a card as possible.

The initiative of these combos is the biggest cost and accordingly it's at its best when used opening a door.

It's typically better to avoid monsters hitting you as much as possible unless you're a true tank class, which the Brute, as a bruiser, is not. You'd rather kill enemies before they attack rather than let a bunch attack you just in order to deal some damage. Additionally, it's very difficult to use on ranged enemies and most enemies in Gloomhaven have the possibility to not attack on their ability cards and thus you can end up wasting a turn doing nothing. Attacking is, by far, more powerful and more consistent in most situations compared to Retaliate and thus Retaliate as the sole focus of an action is certainly best avoided.

The bottom is nothing to get overly excited about but a solid, spammable Move/Heal combo.

Eye for an Eye is not an intrinsically great card mostly because Retaliate is not a good mechanic, but the spammable bottom and decent initiative means it makes it into our starting deck.

Loot is a naturally situational action because you don't want to perform Loot actions in the middle of combat (don't listen to your Scoundrel friend) and most of Gloomhaven will be spent in combat. Although we don't really need a top move having it attached to Loot is an added bonus.

Fortunately, this card is well balanced by pairing this situational top with an extremely spammable bottom. Every class in Gloomhaven appreciates having a couple Move 4's as almost every scenario will require a decent amount of movement. The Brute wants Move 4's more than basically anyone else because of Balanced Measure.

Ah, now Grab and Go is a Loot card.

The top is nice when moving between rooms and coupled with a spammable Move 4.

Finally, this card has an amazing late initiative which will almost always allow you to go after the enemies that round, which naturally pairs perfectly with a Move 4 as you can approach the group of enemies from a long distance (and typically hit them with Balanced Measure as a great opener).

The top is the most consistent AoE Attack in the Brute's arsenal. Skewer definitely has a higher upside if you can hit two targets and power it up, but powering it up requires Leaping Cleave anyway. It's easier to hit two targets with this AoE than it is to hit three targets with Sweeping Blow, both netting the same Attack value while this one is safer and also nets you a free XP.

On the bottom we have a very good Move 3 Jump that creates the aforementioned Air in order to turn on Skewer. Having access to at least one Jump is always helpful both to traverse the map and position where you want in combat.

Leaping Cleave is a great card with a good top and good bottom.

The biggest negatives of this card are the initiative and that you can't play both halves each rest cycle as you really do want them both.

Attack 6 for a loss is pretty far behind the curve - the Scoundrel can deal 5 without a loss on multiple cards very easily and you can actually perform an Attack 6 without a loss with just the use of Boots. Sometimes it might be decent to finish a scenario but you have plenty of better cards that are only slightly worse in that situation and much better the rest of the time, which will set you up to not need something like this at the end.

A Move action with Push is rather clunky for the same reasons mentioned on Sweeping Blow. Admittedly, the Push here is a bit better as pushing one enemy two hexes is more likely to be useful than pushing a few enemies one hex.

Overwhelming Assault is actually just totally underwhelming.

Attack 2 is weak, but Disarm is the second best negative condition in the game and makes this usually worth using at some point. 10 initiative all but guarantees that you'll be able to Disarm the enemy before they attack. The downside is that you're not making a lot of progress towards actually killing enemies when you use this, so it's not great in rooms with a large number of enemies, but instead is much better against smaller numbers of stronger enemies. You also need to be careful who you use this on as some monsters may do something other than attacking, such as healing or summoning.

At the same time, the bottom isn't great. You have to be in a very specific situation to want to use it (you have an ally adjacent to you who is somehow getting attacked instead of you, you don't want to move and you don't have something more effective to do. The addition of the shield means that you are mitigating damage from each attack, but you would usually be better doing something else.

Provoking Roar has a lot going for it. The positives are the top and the initiative, which combine well together.

Despite the fairly weak bottom and the potential weakness of Disarm, this card can still be very effective a good percentage of the time both for disarming strong attackers and even just as a Move 2 with great initiative to allow you to use another top quickly, meaning you'll usually be happy to have it.

Attack 2 is weak but the Stun and XP are good. We will find ourselves using this action a lot.

The bottom Heal is nice along with the Shield. Having non-Move options on bottom is always good, and Shield 1 on early initiative can save you some health. The problem is when is it actually good? In order to use this, you need to be somewhere you're going to get attacked multiple times. But in most of those situations, attacking first and then moving will usually save you more health by virtue of being able to avoid attacks, and movement is always helpful. This might be better against ranged enemies, but against ranged enemies you usually need to constantly be moving in order to close the distance and kill them one by one, and standing still to absorb their attacks only works if you have a ranged Attack to damage them with. You do have one ranged Attack but it's usually going to be used when you enter new rooms and can't make it to enemies rather than when you're already there, which is what this requires.

This card is better than Disarming Roar on account of the Stun and XP. The bottom is also an improvement as a bit of extra healing for when we don't need to move is always welcome even if it doesn't scale well.

This is a card that will stay in our deck for a while.

At its worst, the top here is Attack 3 that gives 1 XP and can reach one hex further. The AoE is more difficult to land consistently on this card than others but can still be done frequently enough when there are multiple enemies. You can even produce your own Air with another level 1 card so you can consistently use this powered-up, making it a very potent Attack indeed.

The bottom is less impressive as you don't really want to lose a card to pay for two more movement most of the time, but on some rare occasions it can still be helpful, and can let you get up to an Attack 8 with Balanced Measure and boots.

Skewer starts the trend of great 'X' cards.

Attack 3, Range 3, 1 XP is a very solid ranged Attack for a 10-card class at level 1. On top of that, just having a single ranged Attack makes a lot of our other cards better and helps a lot with flexibility when entering new rooms.

A bottom attack is always useful, especially for a melee character.

This simple little card does so much work.

While 27 initiative isn't great, it's not terrible, and actually quite decent for the Brute.

Attack 2 is a very small Attack and unlikely to be the difference in killing many enemies. In order for this Attack to provide enough value to be worth playing, you really need to hit 2-3 enemies each time. The problem is that by doing such a small Attack which rarely kills while also positioning yourself adjacent to multiple enemies, this trade often ends up costing you significantly more life than its worth.

Meanwhile, the bottom is also not good. First of all, pushing enemies with a bottom action is a lot worse than pushing them with a top Attack because you typically move next to enemies then perform an Attack for the turn, but if you Push the enemies away, you can only make ranged Attacks, of which you have very few. Additionally, Push 1 is rarely enough to put enemies into traps, which is the primary selling point of Push.

Sweeping Blow is another poor card and this one doesn't even really redeem itself with something like initiative.

Finally, the initiative at 64 is terrible. Early (typically approximately 25 or lower) initiative is very important in Gloomhaven in order to perform your actions before monsters act and late (typically higher than 75) initiative is also quite important in order to set up your turns such that monsters go before you and move toward you without reaching you to perform Attacks. Being right in the middle of the two while still on the late end is really the worst spot possible. 60-75 initiative is generally the worst initiative bracket in the game.

Attack 3, Pierce 2 is great if you are up against shielded enemies and pretty underwhelming if you're not.

The bottom is a good loss at low levels where 2 damage to 2-4 enemies plus moving is worth a loss, but it falls off quickly unless you enhance it, which is expensive for a card you'll only play once per scenario and thus probably not worth it.

Trample is a very fine card but one whose value really depends on the monsters we expect to see in the scenario.

Ultimately, this card is pretty simple to evaluate given that you can see what monsters you'll be up against before you pick your hand for the scenario: if there are enemies with Shield, bring it, if there aren't, don't.

At least pairing Retaliate with Shield makes both of them better but they still have some of the same problems if to a lesser degree and now there's a tremendous risk if the enemies don't end up attacking you.

The bottom is essentially an Attack on bottom but requires at least a two-target AoE to even be an Attack 2, and since your AoEs are melee range, hitting multiple enemies means they're near you and you're not moving away (and often requires you moving to position properly in the first place). In that case, you'd rather be moving away or using something defensive most of the time, not adding one to your Attack. Even Attack 2 on the bottom of Spare Dagger is much better in the aforementioned situation because you have a better chance of eliminating one enemy and only being attacked by one, whereas here, unless the one extra Attack is enough to finish someone off, you're in trouble.

Compounding things, here you have to use this first to get the benefit. For example, if you get an unlucky modifier on your attack with the top attack when using Spare Dagger on the bottom to kill an enemy, you can now, at worst, use Spare Dagger's bottom as a Move 2 (or 4 with boots) to get away and not get pounded. Because you have to use this bottom first, you're doubling down on the risk of getting a bad modifier, which is a very Brute problem if you've ever seen his modifier deck in action.

Wall of Doom is a cool but unfortunately not good card.

The top of Warding Strength is quite good - Attack 3 isn't anything special but with Push 2 you have a reasonable chance to push a monster into a trap on the majority of scenarios. And unlike with bottom Move + Push actions, here you always get to make an Attack first, which is much better on average.

And then the bottom, hmmm... Assuming you take at least 6 more attacks that deal damage in the scenario, this will be a pre-emptive Heal 6 that also gives you 3 experience. That's actually amazing. The problem with healing yourself (or healing in general) while fighting is that you do it instead of something else, which usually means you're not eliminating enemies as quickly as you should be or not avoiding damage, which can negate or diminish the effect of the Heal. By being able to set this up BEFORE you get into a big fight, you get all the upside of healing without the downside. Now because it's a bottom, it's not always easy to get a chance to set this up, but it's usually not that hard.

This card works perfectly because it has a good top: you use the top a few times then use the bottom before the last or second-to-last room.
