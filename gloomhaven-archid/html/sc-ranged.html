Scoundrel Ranged

This build is the ranged build for the scoundrel.

Build Deck

New Cards

The top action is consistently overrated.

The real strength of this card is that it's a move 6, which is our largest move, with an amazing 6 initiative. And let me tell you, you haven't lived until you've played a non-loss move 6 at level 1. It feels great.

You bring this card to scenarios for the bottom action, which is very good, and then if you lose it before the end to a short rest roll, it's really not a big deal.

If you do manage to keep it until the end (which shouldn't be a priority but can just sometimes happen), then sometimes you'll get to play the top loss in the last couple of turns, when playing a loss has a minimal impact on longevity. Otherwise, remember that this loss is just 2 attack higher than our standard attacks, which does not merit an early or mid-scenario loss on power level alone. It can get up to +4 attack with Smoke Bomb, which is a combo we'll try to use on boss fights, but we can never afford two losses outside of those scenarios.

Hitting 5 with adjacency is a lovely action.

Move 5 on the bottom in case we need it is great.

We won't use the bottom much at early levels, preferring to look elsewhere for movement so as to keep this top action, but having the option is still nice.

This is our best level 1 card. Great movement and very low initiative.

Obviously if you can't consistently get the adjacency bonus, this card's top action gets a lot worse, but again you really don't want to play Scoundrel without being able to get adjacency bonuses most of the time, so I don't think that's a serious consideration we need to make.

The move is nice on the top, but the attack in general is underwhelming.

The bottom is also not very good as bottom Loot actions typically remain.

The best thing to do with this card is use the top action alongside the bottom of Thief's Knack to do a combined 5 attack with 2 move in one turn. Obviously that's a lot worse than when we just use one of our attack 5's and get to use a bottom action on top of that but the advantage of this Quick Hands combo is that we don't require allied adjacency on our target, which even in a party with a melee ally, can't always be the case (although in most of those cases, that's when we use our ranged attacks, both because they're more flexible and because it's riskier to approach enemies when we're alone).

Single Out is the same top action as Flanking Strike but worse because of initiative (at least intrinsically) and the bottom action. So there's not a lot to say about the top action, obviously, as it was great on Flanking Strike and it's great here.

The bottom action, on the other hand, you'll again pretty rarely play. The same rule applies regarding average scenarios, but here even on boss scenarios, it's much more questionable because it's harder to use (you need to be sure, usually in advance, that the boss will start and remain isolated for this bottom action to really be worth the loss).

While intrinsically the late initiative might seem bad, having at least one really late initiative is actually very helpful for the Scoundrel, allowing for the classic 'run in late, attack, go early the next around with an attack and move out or finish the enemy.' So even what's 'bad' on this card is often good, making it another very good card.

The strength of this top action largely depends on your party size and to some extent your party composition. First of all, let's establish that you cannot consistently create Dark without enhancement (the Dark enhancement strategy for the Scoundrel is an interesting idea though, to be sure), you only have Smoke Bomb. And we're only using the Smoke Bomb loss on short/boss scenarios, so on those scenarios can this be a better replacement for Backstab when doing a Smoke Bomb combo? If you're in a party of 4, probably. Obviously if you're in a party of 4 and have at least 2 melee allies, then the answer is definitely. Party of 4 with 1 melee ally is close and really ends up being a judgment call based on how much your ranged allies are willing to work with you (can you get at least one of them to move into melee range). If you can get 2 allies in melee range, then this card is obviously better for the combo than Backstab, if you can't, then stick with Backstab.

So it's worthwhile to bring for Smoke Bomb combo scenarios in parties of 4 with 1-2 melee allies. Is it worthwhile for anything other than that? Well, in a 2 player party, never. Obviously the top action can never be good in such a party. Otherwise, if you have an ally who can consistently create Dark, I would absolutely bring this card in a party of 3 or larger. If you don't, I wouldn't, because even in a party of 4, you'd need at least 2 allies next to a target for this even to be an attack 5. And while an attack 5 is good, having 2 allies next to a target and that target not already being dead (obviously excluding bosses, which we addressed above) is quite rare and will usually be far from optimal.

Now, after all this discussion of the top action, how is the bottom? It's fine. A move 3 is obviously on the short end for this class but still on the general scale of actions, a move 3 with a small upside is a nice action. How important is this small upside? Again, it depends on your party composition - if you have someone like a Brute as a melee ally, who typically can't go as early as you, you may need this action in order to setup adjacency some amount of the time. If you have a melee ally like a Mindthief, you'll need this very, very rarely. Finally, if your party completely lacks Push, this action can sometimes work to trigger traps, at least until you get level 7, which gives you much more flexibility in that department.

Sinister Opportunity isn't a bad card but it's also not exceptional as it gives us a very late initiative option.

The top action is not good on an average scenario as you simply cannot afford loss cards that do something like 5-7 attack (potentially even requiring another loss) with a 9 card hand size. On short/boss scenarios, the top is very good and you should usually always bring it for that alone.

Meanwhile, the bottom depends on party composition: if your melee ally is a Mindthief, this action will be pretty unnecessary as the Mindthief can approach enemies at similar initiatives to you without a problem.

If your melee ally is a Brute, this action will actually be quite good as you can reposition enemies next to the Brute before the Brute goes, which is an issue for him as he can rarely go as early as you in the round. If you have no melee allies this action is also quite good but I wouldn't recommend that situation in general.

This is a very situational card and depends on both the scenario and party composition.

If you're in combat, the bottom functions a bit like a non-move bottom action, giving at least +1 damage for free, while still allowing you to move, if necessary.

Special Mixture is a very nice card and is consistently quite underrated.

Being able to use traps as a ranged attack is a very powerful mechanic, especially against shielded enemies and archers who drop traps as an action.

The downside for you is that you don't typically want to spend a turn next to an enemy in order to be able to use this action the following turn.

Thief's Knack has two good, if somewhat situation sides.

The downside for you is that you don't typically want to spend a turn next to an enemy in order to be able to use this action the following turn. That leaves two common situations in which this attack is good:

  1. with the top action of Quick Hands (and later the level 3 card) which allows you to move in and still get the bottom.
  2. it's quite common for melee classes to try to go as late as possible in order to allow enemies to act without reaching them (assuming the enemies don't have enough movement or range to do so - this strategy falls apart significantly as you get to the higher levels when enemies have way too much movement or range to avoid) and then walk in late with an attack, followed by a very early action the following round.

    3. Typically when the Scoundrel does this routine it's part of an in-and-out dance where you run back outside of the reach of monsters the following turn, but sometimes you'll just need a little extra damage to finish an enemy off, so you can play a top and bottom attack early the next round, using the bottom of Thief's Knack, and get in for something like 8 damage to eliminate an enemy (and thus you may not need to run away as an attacker should be gone). Obviously the risk with this strategy is that you're extremely exposed should you flip a miss.

First of all, how good is this top action? Very, very good for a level 1 card (for comparison, the Spellweaver gets literally the exact same top action, on a playable card, at level 4 with 20 initiative). For turns where you don't want to or aren't sure that you can get next to enemies with a melee ally, you can toss some knives and barely miss out on any damage (4 vs 5, but obviously scales better with your modifier deck). The only downside is that your job will typically be eliminating enemies before they go and instead spreading a smaller amount of damage around on multiple targets won't always feel great as you may be leaving all the enemy attackers around.

Meanwhile, you'll pretty much never play the bottom action of this card as it's a loss and while a Loot 2 loss is also quite powerful at level 1 (the Spellweaver gets that same action at level 3), you'll eventually have enhanced Swift Bow (or your boots) for looting most of the time and losses are too painful.

Throwing Knives seems out of place as a multi-target ranged attack on a melee assassin but the Scoundrel has a knife-throwing subtheme and honestly, we're quite happy to see it because it provides some welcome flexibility.

If somehow this ends up being one of your last 2-3 cards at the end of a scenario (which isn't typically ideal but can happen), then you may occasionally get to fire off the bottom.

The top action has the potential to be very powerful against high-shield enemies that are otherwise difficult to kill. Still, it is a loss, which means we'll basically only bring this card when facing Flame Demons or Vermling Shaman in shorter scenarios (and we should typically save it for an elite).

The bottom action is fine and you will use it sometimes when you bring this card for the top but is not enough to justify bringing the card on a regular basis by itself. The problem is that you can only use this action when you're already in range for an attack, which means primarily with ranged attacks or if you ended your turn in melee range of an enemy (but in the case of the latter, you'll generally prefer to use Thief's Knack to help finish an enemy).

Trickster's Reversal is a pretty bad card that fits firmly into the sideboard card category.

The top action is good for when you need to lead the way in attacking an enemy and for whatever reason can't rely on an ally to get there before you. By giving an attack 3 poison, you'll often end up doing around an attack 5 with this attack anyway as Poison should add around 2 additional attack in this situations.

When you don't need the top attack (which will be more often than not, ideally), you've just got a casual move 5 on the bottom.

Venom Shiv is a fine-but-not-exciting card.

The biggest downside of this card is the initiative, which is not good. While having 1-2 very late initiatives can be very good, 60 is late without being late enough to actually be good.
