Nightshroud Shadow

Overview

Welcome to my guide to the Nightshroud, my personal favourite character and arguably the most powerful in the game (other leading contenders being specific builds of the Sun and Music Note). Properly designed, playing this character is the Gloomhaven equivalent of God-mode, as you can basically ignore all monster attacks, damage, statuses, hit points, shields, and...pretty much everything else that creates challenges for your mortal companions.

On a related note, and as a preface to the strategy discussion, it's worth briefly discussing some metagame considerations for this character. In addition to being enormously powerful, the Nightshroud is mostly a loner, which tends to reduce the amount of teamwork and coordination required with your companions. Support characters, in particular, may end up feeling a bit left out of the action when you need no healing, use no buffs, are indifferent to monster statuses, and (for the most part) hit points. In addition, your invulnerability stems from your ability to near-constantly remain invisible, which will ensure that whichever monsters you haven't yet killed will concentrate fire on your companions. Properly played, this won't necessarily make you a 'bad' teammate, as you will be shielding them (as an obstacle) and dropping bodies with great effectiveness; however, there's some risk your teammates will end up feeling less than thrilled in the end. I've played this class for ~10 scenarios now, and consider myself fortunate that my friends were very good sports about the whole thing.

Strengths

With that out of the way, let's review the few core strengths of this character. He has the abilities to:
  1. Be invisible, almost always
  2. Consume Dark to automatically kill, or do massive damage, without losing cards
  3. Manipulate initiative to an extraordinary extent (which is one of the keys to point 1)
  4. Jam doors, shielding teammates from all monsters without ranged attacks and/or flying
  5. Regularly gain 20+ XP per scenario (before scenario bonus XP)

Challenges

On the flip side, the Nightshroud also faces a few challenges:
  1. Inflexibility: accomplishing all of the aforementioned lofty goals comes at a cost: one must plan carefully in advance more than any other class, such that every single card played, in the correct order, is a cog in a complex machine. (Ironically, this will result in some of our actions being quite underwhelming in the short-term, as they are intended primarily as setup for a later nova action.) Death incarnate needs to keep to a strict schedule.
  2. Card count / Loss actions: With a hand of only 9 cards, each one is precious and playing cards as losses will risk early exhaustion. Also, losing cards prematurely is tantamount to needlessly removing cogs from the previously-mentioned complex machine, which can cause serious problems.
  3. Movement: This one will get a lot better as we level up, buy enhancements, and acquire equipment, but out of the gate at level 1 this character is possibly the slowest in the game.
  4. Resting: Related to the first point, the Nightshroud can almost never afford to short rest, as removing a cog from the machine at random is extremely risky. During a long-rest will, incidentally, be one of the few times you are at risk of being hit, unless you get help from an item or ally to turn invisible again.
  5. Traps: For the first several levels, this class has no access to jump movements or push/pull attacks, and can't afford to waste money or body/enhancement slots on a jump item or enhancement. That said, we have a surprisingly large health pool (medium progression) and almost never get hit, so I quickly became our party's resident expert at disarming traps for the group (using my face). For the Nightshroud, hit points (friend and foe) are just a state of mind.

Strategy

Before we review specific cards, it's important to establish the framework for what we are trying to accomplish with each of our actions:

This is a summary of what we are aspiring to accomplish, for each action of the game. Note that this is idealized, and much of it will only become possible at higher levels and/or through the use of items and/or help from teammates (the turn 3 bottom, in particular, will never work perfectly without items and/or help).

As a first rules reminder, invisibility expires at the END of your turn after turning invisible, so you can still use it on the second turn after you turn invisible.

Corollary to that, manipulation of initiative is critical to maximize your invisibility, and is therefore key to maximizing your effectiveness. Often, people think of 'good' and 'bad' initiative as synonymous with 'early' and 'late', which is far from the case (for all classes). In almost all situations, you have a preference for whether you want to go before or after the monsters, and choosing your initiative is your way of forcing the monsters to play in your desired order. Therefore, 'good' initiative means a mix of extremes to enable the player to choose to act first or last, with 'bad' initiative cards residing in the middle (playing initiative 50 is effectively ceding control of the order to a coin toss). Fortunately, the Nightshroud happens to be among the best in the game in that area, with some cards that are very early and others that are very late; monsters causing you problems by acting out of order will be a rare occurrence indeed. In particular, this will enable you to consistently turn invisible before the monsters act in a round, and not reappear until after they have already wasted their following turn.

As a second rules reminder, you cannot consume an element that you generated in the same turn, but you CAN consume an element while simultaneously generating it for the following turn. The Nightshroud will very often consume and generate Dark on the same turn.

Resting

One must indeed LONG rest, to avoid prematurely losing the wrong card (later in a scenario, it matters a bit less, though still preferable to long rest if you can).

Second, following the blueprint above will leave you exposed (IE, visible, like any mortal character) during your rest, but there are a few factors that make this less of a big deal:

  • Hopefully, most of the monsters in the room are dead by now
  • You'll heal 2 hp at the end of your long rest, and generally already be at full health
  • Your companion(s) will be acting on earlier initiative than you, and therefore will be preferentially targeted by the monsters if you are equidistant
  • If all else fails, you should have 1-4 items that can also turn you invisible, which you can always spend at the end of turn 4 if the outlook is bleak (though it is ideal to conserve those items to plug holes in your combos once you get down to 4-6 cards

Third, let's discuss the correct order in which we want to lose cards, to ensure that things continue to run as smoothly as possible.


The first card to lose is obviously whichever 9th card you brought into the adventure (generally this will be an inferior bottom Dark generator which you'll swap in for just the first cycle, but more detail on that below). After that, I would usually lose Doomed Breeze, then Cloak of Shade, meaning that the turn 4 attack gets downgraded to attack 6 instead of attack 8, unless you use an item or get help from an ally to turn invisible.

Tactical Points

Door Jambing

Being invisible all the time has the potential to make your teammates lives more challenging, as monsters will focus fire on them instead. Fortunately, there is one major way that you can thank them for putting up with your shenanigans: HOld the DOoR. You should nearly always be the one opening doors, ideally on the first or third turns in your cycle (IE, on a turn when you have just gone INTO invisibility, vs one where you just came out of it), since the monsters in the new room will always get a turn after you open the door. Your tactics from there will be dictated by the types of monsters in the room, but in all cases depend upon you and your teammate(s) aggressively manipulating initiative:
  1. Ranged monsters: These can still harm your teammates, but only if they get close enough. For these, your teammates should do their best to mirror your usual initiative trick, except that instead of invisibility they are using their cadence for movement (IE, start off out of range, act late to move into range, attack, then act early to attack again and then move back out of range). If your teammates do it properly, the monsters will move right up next to you in order to get as close as they can to within range of your teammates, at which point you will murder them (the monsters, that is).
  2. Flying monsters: These end up behaving similarly to ranged enemies, but mostly bypass your door-jamming. However, in order to do that, they have to fly directly over you, so batter up! When they land near you, kill them.
  3. Melee monsters: This is where your shielding will really shine, as your party can just ignore them altogether until you've finished killing the monsters in the other 2 categories. If you want to really err on the safe side, you can manipulate initiative to move in the same way as your teammates, and go 'hunting' every second turn before returning to your post on the door (IE, start on the door, act late and move off, kill a monster, act early, attack another monster and then move back to the door).

Looting

We have no loot 2 cards, no reusable loot 1 cards (until later when you level up), and slow movement. However, we do have unparalleled ability to expedite monsters' transition from nuisance to loot, and we prefer to move on every turn. Therefore, a common sequence (particularly at higher levels when we have better movement to setup in advance of our attacks) is to attack first, then take the newly-vacated monster's spot.

Throughout an adventure, I was usually able to pull that off ~ 5 times, then would grab some additional loot here and there (since you will generally be in the thick of the fight for your melee attacks) due to moving every turn. All told, I was usually bringing in a double digit number of loot tokens in a decent adventure. Also, in your role as steward of doors, you'll often end up with a large pileup of loot right next to yourself, as progressively more monsters advance themselves on a suicidal conveyor belt trying to shoot/fly at your allies.

No guide would be complete without a discussion of perks. The good news is that our perk deck doesn't matter very much, since most of our best offense comes from kill effects that don't flip cards. The bad news is that those Silent Force attacks will be stuck with probably the worst set of perks in the game.

We start with the only surprisingly good perk, after that, we have an OK batch that are sometimes useful:

Greater ImmunityIgnore negative scenario effects and add 2x +1
EnshroudAdd +1 Invisible
SweepAdd rolling add target
IncantationAdd 2x rolling Curse

Before I cover the last (awful) batch of perks, we need to take a step back to think about how our attacks differ from most. Unlike a typical DPS or AOE class that wants to make the most out of a larger number of 3-4 attacks, we only make one real attack per cycle, with Silent Force (technically yes we also get a puny attack 1-2 with Wings of the Night or Grim Sustenance, and sometimes with Doomed Breeze, but those are so pathetic that designing our deck around them is the tail wagging the dog). [Refer also to the Prosperity 3 item spoiler section for another point that further reinforces this situation]. The effective value of the 'Null' and '2x' cards in most decks is thus 3-4, by either negating or doubling the attack 3-4. For us, however, this is not the case. The 'Null' is worth a whopping -8 (or the remaining hp of the monster, if lower), and the 2x is usually more like +0-2 if the monster would be dead anyway (or narrowly survive with 1-2 hp). In rare cases, like a boss fight, the 2x will give us full credit for an awesome +8, but those situations are rare (again, for the big 16 hp monster, we are just executing anyway, so there is a selection bias in our attacks). This is a long way of highlighting a simple fact: for this class, pulling the 'Null' card is a total disaster, so we need to be careful about taking perks that thin out our deck, thereby increasing the risk of pulling the 'Null'. In fact, in certain scenarios with a large number of ~6 hp enemies (or those with 6 hp enemies surrounding 1-2 big bads that you'll outright execute), which will die even by pulling the -1 or -2 cards, we would prefer to add an infinite number of -1's to the deck, such that we always pull -1 (killing the monster anyway) and never get the Null.
